AM/FM Radio

Television is not the only source of news and entertainment that uses radio transmissions that are free to receive. AM and FM radio predate television considerably and are both still very much alive.

Please note that if you are only interested in FM radio, things are somewhat simpler. The FM band falls between VHF-Lo and VHF-Hi and most modern radio tuners with antenna input use the same 75Ω coaxial female F-connector antenna jack that ATSC television uses.

This allows the same splitters and signal amplifiers that work with OTA television to also work with FM broadcast.

In fact for FM radio, if your antenna—such as many but not all Yagi-Uda antennas—works with both VHF-Lo and VHF-Hi there is a good possibility it also works well for FM, at least for FM broadcast from the same direction for which the antenna is pointed.

In theory you can use a combiner to combine the output of an FM antenna with your television antenna but in practice that is not very realistic. ATSC tuners do not decode FM signals (analog or digital) so you would lose about 4 dB when combining the signal only to lose about 4 dB again when you split the signal to feed both an ATSC tuner and an FM tuner. That is a 7–8 dB loss.

If you also want AM radio, AM broadcasts from 550–1720 kHz (0.55–1.72 MHz) which unfortunately is well below the operational range for amplifiers and splitters that are used for OTA television.

This appendix chapter will discuss setting up an AM/FM antenna distributed to multiple receivers via coaxial cable along with notes about setting up a similar system that is FM only.

Test Environment

An inexpensive ‘Antop AT-212FM Curved Panel Amplified Indoor AM/FM Antenna’ connected directly to the test receiver will be used as a reference antenna. I chose that as my reference antenna because it is a decent quality yet inexpensive indoor antenna with better reception than the junk that typically ships with a receiver. For many people it may be good enough. This guide is about saving money, not geek pride.

My analog only Yamaha R-S201 receiver will be used to test how many stations on both bands are received, and how well they come in. As such digital AM/FM reception will not be tested.

During all tests except for the final test my PC, television, and WiFi router will be turned off as they are all possible sources of interference. The final test will test the performance of the antenna system with those possible sources of interference turned on, but television muted.

Due to physical logistics, the tests can not be performed all on the same day so it is possible atmospheric difference can impact the results. All tests will be performed between the hours of 1:00 A.M. and 3:00 A.M. when reception is typically very good.

Antenna Selection

If you are only interested in distributing FM through a coaxial system to multiple receivers, there are many choices including models that often do well in an attic, though installation on a roof top is generally better.

If you are trying to receive a very distant FM station, a directional antenna installed on your roof is the best bet. Generally, an FM antenna distributed to several receivers should probably be omni-directional.

Some models—such as the horizontal folded loop antennas that often work well in an attic—are only mostly omnidirectional and you might have to play with the direction to find the best reception position but generally you can leave it once you have found the sweet spot.

Mono-pole ‘whip’ antennas mounted vertically are truly omnidirectional but are less likely to do as well inside an attic though sometimes they do just fine.

For AM in addition to FM, the only choice I found that works well distributed through a coaxial cable system is the Pixel Technologies AFHD-4 kit. It is a mono-pole ‘whip’ antenna with a 48 inch rod. It could be installed in some attics but the height of the rod limits the locations in within an attic which may make it difficult to install away from sources of interference, such as the HVAC system or solar related equipment. AM reception is particularly impacted by noise.

Note that the AFHD-4 kit is just for AM/FM reception. If you want SiriusXM as well, they have kits to facilitate SiriusXM in addition to AM/FM with that antenna. SiriusXM however is beyond the scope of this guide.

The AFHD-4 kit is what I am using for the tests.

Antenna Location

Generally the higher the antenna, the better the reception will be. However there are some considerations.

The highest possible install point at our home would be the chimney. The chimney is also farthest from possible most sources of noise at our home. However the chimney is on the south-east side of the home and the grounding rod is on the north-west side of the home. Properly earthing the antenna would not be possible.

The next best location height-wise would be at the roof peak on the west side of the home. It could be earthed, but installing the mast and antenna would be expensive because it would require a professional installer.

There are some fake windows along the north side of the roof. Properly earthing the antenna may not be possible, I am not sure. It also could be considered an eye-sore generating complaints from neighbors.

The location I chose is on the west side of the house where the house roof meets the garage roof.

This location is fairly close to the grounding rod. it is set back from the street so that the antenna is not as visible from the street. The required ladder is reasonable. It is also close to two of the three rooms that will have AM/FM receivers. There is also already an existing mast for a TV sattelite that we do not use.

The downside is that there is a fluorescent ballast in the garage and it is fairly close to our wireless router, both potential sources of AM interference.

Splitting the Signal

If you are only interested in FM, you can use the same kind of splitters or amplified distributors you would use with a television install. For FM only I would recommend just using a regular amplified distributor.

The frequency used by AM however is way too low for those devices. You must use splitters specifically designed for 0.5 MHz at the bottom end. Also, if you are distributing SiriusXM as well, you need a splitter than handles up to 2400 MHz. Many splitters / aplified distributors for television do but some only handle up to about 1000 MHz.

The Pixel Technologies MBS-4 is designed specifically for the 0.5–2400 MHz frequency range.

Note that splitting the signal will almost certainly require amplification. The necessary amplifier for the AM band does not have a separate input for power, so one of the outputs on that splitter will need to be used for amplification.

AM Compatible Inline Amp

Amplifiers used for broadcast and sattelite televion WILL NOT WORK for an AM radio signal.

To amplify a coaxial signal carrying AM radio, use the Pixel Technologies MBA-12 Inline Amp.

That amp should be placed as close to the antenna as possible to reduce the amplification of noise that enters the signal after the signal leaves the antenna.

To power that amp, you will need a Pixel Technologies PS-1 Power Inserter. That adapter connects via coaxial cable to one of the splitter outputs.

Please note that the power inserter is 5 volts. The 12 volt adapters sometimes used with television preamplifiers and amplified distributors is too much voltage for the MBA-12 Inline Amp.
